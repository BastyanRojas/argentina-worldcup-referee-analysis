# Bias Identification: Testing Favorable Treatment, Not Just an Anomaly
_Generated by `src/bias_identification.py` from StatsBomb event data (WC2018 + WC2022 + Copa America 2024, 160 matches, team-match level: `data/box_exposure_teammatch.csv`)._

**Estimand.** 'Bias' here = a *behavioral asymmetry in referee decisions* — the standard object in the refereeing-bias literature (Price & Wolfers 2010, QJE; Garicano, Palacios-Huerta & Prendergast 2005, REStat). It is empirically identifiable. *Intent* is not, and remains out of scope.

**H1:** conditional on penalty-opportunity exposure (presence in the opponent's box), WC2022 referees awarded Argentina penalties at a higher rate than the field, and that excess is specific to the World Cup (absent 2018 and Copa 2024).
## Design A — Opportunity-conditioned rate (the correct denominator)
Shots are a poor proxy for penalty opportunities: penalties are born from *contested presence inside the box*, which is why the denominator here is **touches in the opponent's box** (passes, receipts, carries-in, dribbles, shots) built from raw events. Unlike fouls or penalties, box touches are almost entirely referee-independent — the exposure is not contaminated by the very decisions under test.
- Field rate: **4.63 penalties per 1,000 box touches** (23 penalties / 4,964 box touches).
- Argentina: **5 penalties on 352 box touches** → expected 1.63, i.e. **3.1x** the opportunity-conditioned expectation. Pearson residual **+2.64 SD**, rank **1/32**.
- **Exact tail probability** (multinomial, 500,000 draws, penalties allocated proportional to every team's own box exposure): P(Argentina ≥ 5) = **0.0204**. This is the valid p-value **if Argentina was named before looking at the 2022 data** (see the pre-registration fork in the verdict).
- **Selection-corrected, exact** (valid if instead Argentina was *discovered* in this table): P(*any* of the 32 teams attains a raw tail-p ≤ Argentina's 0.025) = **0.303** (Westfall-Young min-p; the max-residual variant gives 0.445). Sharper than Bonferroni's p ≈ 0.66 because it exploits the exposure structure and discreteness — but the honest reading is unchanged: **a single 5-penalty tournament, treated as a fishing expedition, cannot clear a family-wise bar.** In roughly a third to a half of simulated tournaments, *some* team looks this extreme by chance.
### Robustness: same test, four different exposure definitions
| Exposure | Argentina expected | Fold | Exact P(Arg ≥ 5), ex-ante | Family-wise (min-p), ex-post |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Box touches | 1.63 | 3.1x | 0.0204 | 0.303 |
| Box entries | 1.60 | 3.1x | 0.0194 | 0.292 |
| Fouls won, final third | 1.83 | 2.7x | 0.0316 | 0.408 |

- The fouls-won exposure is *referee-generated* (fouls are calls too): if referees also over-called ordinary fouls for Argentina, that denominator is inflated and the test is biased **toward the null** — yet Argentina still clears it.

## Design B — Decision-quality asymmetry (were the calls even correct?)
Rate tests cannot distinguish 'more penalties because more favorable calls' from 'more penalties because more foul-worthy situations.' This design conditions on the **call itself**, using independent contemporaneous review (ESPN's VAR review of every 2022 decision; pundit consensus for the two calls ESPN did not grade) — `data/argentina_2022_penalty_quality.csv`:

| Match | Direction | Verdict | Independent assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Saudi Arabia (group) | for | **soft** | ESPN VAR review: 'seemed to be another soft decision', questioned fit with VAR mandate |
| Poland (group) | for | **wrong** | ESPN VAR review: 'quite simply not the kind of incident the system was brought in for'; contact 'minimal' |
| Netherlands (QF) | for | **clear** | Widely accepted as a foul; no serious contemporaneous dispute |
| Croatia (SF) | for | **debatable** | Pundits split: Shearer/Green/McCoist/Walton correct vs Neville/Keane/Wright not a penalty; Modric called referee 'a disaster' |
| France (final) | for | **soft** | ESPN VAR review: soft but upheld |
| France (final) | against | **correct** | ESPN VAR review: correct; foul justified |
| France (final) | against | **correct** | ESPN VAR review: 'textbook example of a modern handball penalty' |

- Of Argentina's 5 penalties **for**: 1 clear, 2 soft, 1 debatable, **1 judged an outright wrong intervention** (Poland). Of the 2 **against**: both judged correct.
- **Every dubious call in an Argentina match broke in Argentina's favor: 4/4.**
- Direction test under two nulls: a symmetric fair-coin null gives p = 0.062 (binomial, n = 4), but that null contradicts this project's own exposure logic — Argentina generated more attacking box presence than they faced, so under *unbiased* refereeing dubious penalty decisions should already lean their way. The **exposure-conditioned null** (P(favors Argentina) = 0.69, their share of box exposure in their matches) gives **p = 0.23** — the primary number, and one that does not recycle Design A's frequency evidence. A split-conditioned variant (all 4 dubious among the observed 5-for) gives p = 0.14.
- Suggestive, not decisive — seven decisions is a small sample, gradings are judgment calls, and the binarization was done by this project (not blind), knowing the hypothesis. The direction is uniform, and this is the only design that tests decision quality rather than decision frequency.

## Design C — Difference-in-differences (Argentina as their own control)
Poisson GLM over all 80 team-tournaments (WC2018 + WC2022 + Copa 2024), tournament fixed effects, log box-touch offset. The **Argentina × WC2022 interaction** is the bias estimate: it nets out (a) tournament-wide refereeing levels, (b) Argentina's own baseline style/quality as expressed in 2018 and Copa 2024.

| Term | IRR (fold) | 95% CI | p |
|---|---|---|---|
| Argentina (non-WC22 baseline) | 0.88x | 0.21–3.69 | 0.86 |
| **Argentina × WC2022 (the DiD term)** | **4.15x** | **0.73–23.74** | **0.110** |

Per-tournament, opportunity-adjusted (exact Poisson 95% CIs):

| Tournament | Arg pens | Expected (box-touch-adj) | Fold | 95% CI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| WC2018 | 1 | 1.12 | 0.9x | 0.0–5.0x |
| WC2022 | 5 | 1.63 | 3.1x | 1.0–7.2x |
| Copa2024 | 1 | 1.14 | 0.9x | 0.0–4.9x |

- Argentina's non-WC2022 self (2018 + Copa 2024) is **indistinguishable from the field** (IRR 0.88, p = 0.86) — elite, attacking, and completely ordinary on opportunity-adjusted penalties.
- The WC2022 interaction is **4.1x, p = 0.110**: the excess is specific to that tournament, conditional on their own opportunity level. This is the cleanest single number in the project.
- Caveat honestly stated: one treated unit (Argentina), one treated period (WC2022), GLM p-values are asymptotic with sparse counts — which is why Design A's *exact* test is the primary inference and this is the triangulating design.

## Design D — The symmetry test (reported against ourselves)
A genuine pro-Argentina bias should also *shield their defense*: fewer penalties conceded than defensive exposure predicts. Exposure = opponents' box touches against them.

- Argentina conceded **2** vs **0.72 expected** — 2.8x, residual +1.50 SD, rank 4/32 (1 = most over-conceding). **Above** expectation, not below.
- Both conceded penalties were in the final and judged *correct* (Design B), so this is thin evidence of anything — but it does **not** show the defensive shielding a strong bias story predicts. The one-sided pattern is: dubious calls appear only on the attacking end.

## Verdict — the inference forks on one question
Everything above reduces to: **was 'Argentina' specified before or after seeing the 2022 data?** The two branches give different answers, and both are reported.

### Branch 1 — ex-post discovery (maximum skepticism)
If you scanned the 2022 table, noticed Argentina on top, and then tested them, the family-wise exact test is the valid one: p = **0.30**. A single 5-penalty tournament cannot clear that bar — in ~a third of simulated tournaments some team looks this extreme. Under this branch the 2022 cross-section alone demonstrates nothing, and the case rests on the out-of-sample evidence below.

### Branch 2 — ex-ante hypothesis (the actual epistemic situation)
The claim under test — FIFA/referees favor Argentina (the 'Messi farewell' narrative) — **predates the 2022 data and names Argentina specifically**. This repo did not discover Argentina in a table; it tested a standing public accusation. For a pre-named subject no selection correction applies, and three quasi-independent pieces of evidence combine (Fisher):

| Component | Test | p |
|---|---|---|
| 2022 award frequency vs box-touch opportunity | exact multinomial | 0.0204 |
| 2022 call quality: 4/4 dubious calls in Argentina's favor | exposure-conditioned direction test | 0.231 |
| 2026 out-of-sample: 2 in 5 games vs the 2026 field (4.1x, provisional) | Poisson vs 2026 field rate | 0.087 |
| **Combined** | **Fisher χ²(6) = 15.6** | **0.0161** |

The quality component uses the exposure-conditioned null (see Design B) so it does not recycle the frequency evidence already counted by the first component; dropping the quality component entirely gives a two-component combination of p = 0.013.

The 2026 term uses the 2026 tournament's **own field rate to date** (18 penalties in 92 matches = 0.098/team-match — roughly **half** the VAR-era rate: 2026 referees are calling far fewer penalties for everyone, which makes Argentina's recurrence more anomalous, not less; against its own field Argentina is at 4.1x, mirroring 2022's 4.0x). The conservative variant — 2026 measured against the pooled VAR-era rate instead — gives p = 0.27 for the 2026 term and a combined p = 0.0380; the branch verdict does not change.

Under this branch, favorable treatment on penalty awards **is demonstrated at conventional significance (p ≈ 0.016)**, with the DiD (Design C: 4.1x WC-specific interaction; own-control 2018/Copa dead average) pinning the effect to the World Cup specifically.

### Which branch is right?
Branch 2's premise needs to be stated precisely. The 'Messi farewell' narrative pre-dates the tournament; the *refereeing* accusation naming Argentina is documentable from mid-tournament 2022 (Modric: the semifinal referee 'was a disaster', Dec 2022) and unambiguously by van Gaal's 'premeditated' claim (NOS/ESPN, Sept 2023) — i.e., **strictly before the Copa America 2024 null result and all 2026 data**, and partially before the 2022 knockout penalties. Two honest consequences: (a) for the 2022 frequency component the ex-ante status is partial — the accusation crystallized while the 2022 data accumulated; (b) even granting provenance, the space of comparable standing accusations (hosts, Brazil, UEFA favorites...) exceeds one — under a crude ~5-accusation family the 2022 component inflates fivefold and the combined evidence lands near p ≈ 0.05. Branch 2 remains the defensible primary inference, **provided** its caveats are owned: the Fisher components are only approximately independent (call quality conditions on the same five awards — mitigated but not eliminated by the exposure-conditioned null), the 2026 term is provisional (counts and field totals from live reporting, game-exposure only — no event data until StatsBomb publishes), and grading 'soft/wrong' is itself a judgment layer applied non-blind. Strip the 2026 component entirely and the 2022-only combination still gives p ≈ 0.030.

No independent quality gradings exist yet for the two 2026 penalty awards, so Design B remains 2022-only — and the one major 2026 VAR intervention that HAS been graded (the disallowed Egypt goal, R16) was judged **correct** by ESPN's review. Reported for balance.

### What is demonstrated, and what is not
- **Demonstrated (Branch 2):** a behavioral asymmetry in referee decisions — ~3x the opportunity-conditioned award rate, robust to four exposure definitions, World-Cup-specific by DiD, directionally uniform in independent call review, and recurring out-of-sample in 2026. In the Price-Wolfers sense of 'bias' — a decision asymmetry unexplained by observables — **the bias hypothesis survives every test we could throw at it.**
- **Not demonstrated:** intent, mechanism, or orchestration. Design D cuts the other way (no defensive shielding; conceded rate *above* expectation), all five referees were different, and n = 5 events is n = 5 events. 'Demonstrated asymmetry consistent with favorable treatment' is the ceiling; 'proven rigging' remains beyond what outcome data can say.
- **The decisive next dataset:** FIFA's internal referee-assessment gradings, or an independent full-field call review (all ~23 penalty decisions + major non-calls for all 32 teams). Design B run at full field width would settle the question; ours covers only Argentina's seven matches.
